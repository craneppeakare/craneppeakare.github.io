[Summary] Ultralearning by Scott H. Young

This is a no-bullshit summary of the book “Ultralearning” by Scott H. Young.

Before getting to the 9 principles of what makes an effective Ultralearner, Schott H. Young prefaces by explaining the manic-level obbession that many ultralearners exhibit to their chosen skill. They obsess over every tiny efficiency and detail, and while this degree of obsession may be difficult to achieve for a skill meant just as a simple hobby or to promote skills beneficial to your work force, learning their principles can help you become a more effective learning for anything you may wish to pursue in your life.

“Ultralearning isn’t easy. It’s hard and frustrating and requires stretching outside the limits of where you feel comfortable.”

Now without further ado, here are the 9 principles explained in the book:

Principle 1: Metalearning

Start by mapping out the necessary components of the skill you wish to learn. Discover the most effective strategies, map out each area of the skill, and finally execute the plan.

Answering WHY?: The Expert Interview Method

Answering WHAT?: There are 3 ‘categories’ of skills:

1. Concepts
2. Facts
3. Kinesthetics

Answering HOW?: The Emphasize/Exclude Method

The 10% Rule: Only invest approximately 10% of your total expected learning time into metalearning.

Principle 2: Focus

Make time to practice and how to avoid distraction in order to use that time effectively.

Problem 1: Failing to Start Focusing

Problem 2: Failing to Sustain Focus

- Disctraction source 1: Your Environment
- Disctraction source 2: Your Task
- Disctraction source 3: Your Mind

Problem 3: Failing to Create the Right Kind of Focus

Principle 3: Directness

The best learning comes from doing exactly the thing you want to get good at. Over and over.

“The Problem of Transfer”

How to Learn Directly:

1. Project-Based Learning
2. Immersive Learning
3. The Flight Simulator Method
4. The Overkill Approach

Principle 4: Drill

Breakup complex skills into smaller and smaller parts in order to build up.

Drill 1: Time Slicing
Drill 2: Cognitive Components
Drill 3: The Copycat
Drill 4: The Magnifying Glass Method
Drill 5: Prerequisite Chaining

Principle 5: Retrieval

Practice active recall and consolidate a strong understanding in order to never forget your skill.

How to Practice retrieval:

1. Flash Cards
2. Free Recall
3. Question-Book Method
4. Self-generated Challenges
5. Closed-book learning

Principle 6: Feedback

Get feedback from experts or your audience. Types of feedback and the best way to use each type of feedback.

1. Outcome Feedback: Tells you how well you're doing overall, but no idea on how to do better.
2. Informational Feedback: Tells you what you're doing wrong, but not how to fix it.
3. Corrective Feedback: What's wrong AND how to fix it.

Improve the quality of your feedback

Tactic 1: Noice Cancellation
Tactic 2: Hitting the Difficulty Sweet Spot
Tactic 3: Metafeedback
Tactic 4: High-Intensity, Rapid Feedback

Principle 7: Retention

Ways to efficiently move you skill into long-term memory so you never forget.

3 reasons why people forget:

1. Decay: Forgetting with time
2. Interference: Overwriting Old Memories with New Ones
3. Forgotten Cues: A Locked Box with No Key

Ways to prevent forgetting:

1. Spaced Repition Systems
2. Proceduralization (AKA The Chunking Method)
3. Overlearning
4. Mnemonics

Principle 8: Intuition

Develop a deep understanding of concepts and skills. Start to feel and know your skill rather than cheap memorization.

Rule 1: Don't give up on hard problems easily
Rule 2: Prove things to unerstand them
Rule 3: Always start with a concrete example
Rule 4: Don't fool yourself

The Feynman Technique:

1. Write down the concept or problem on a piece of paper
2. Either convey the idea or explain the problem and its solution to someone as if they have never heard it before.
3. When you get stuck and fail to provide a clear answer, that is your cue to go back and study that part in order to find the answer.

Principle 9: Experimentation

A call to arms to experiment with the way you personally learn best. Use the previous principles as a starting point.

3 types of Experimentation

1. Experiment with Learning Resources
2. Experiment with Technique
3. Experiment with Style

How to Experiment

1. Copy, then Create
2. Compare Methods Side-by-side
3. Introduce New Constraints
4. Find Your Superpower in the Hybrid of Unrelated Skills
5. Explore the Extremes
